Section 1.1

What is Business Analytics?

Definition

Business Analytics is the use of data, information technology, statistical analysis, quantitative methods, and mathematical or computer-based models to help managers gain improved insight about their business operations and make better, fact-based decisions.

Key Components

  • Data collection and preparation
  • Information technology
  • Statistical analysis
  • Quantitative methods
  • Mathematical or computer-based models

Benefits of Business Analytics

Reduced Costs

Optimize operations and eliminate waste

Better Risk Management

Identify and mitigate potential risks

Faster Decisions

Reduce decision-making time with data-driven insights

Improved Performance

Enhanced profitability and customer satisfaction

Challenges

Lack of understanding of how to use analytics
Competing business priorities
Insufficient analytical skills
Difficulty in getting good data and sharing information
Misunderstanding the benefits versus perceived costs

Real-World Example: Netflix

Netflix is a prime example of a company that leverages business analytics to drive decision-making and gain competitive advantage.

  • Uses viewing data from 200+ million subscribers to analyze patterns
  • Predicts which shows will be popular with specific audience segments
  • Personalizes recommendations for each user to increase engagement
  • Makes data-driven decisions on content creation, investing billions in original content based on analytics

Through effective business analytics, Netflix has transformed from a DVD rental service to one of the world's leading entertainment companies.

  • Simulation and Risk Analysis

  • Decision Support Systems (DSS)

    Interactive systems that aid in decision-making processes

    Presenting data in visual formats to facilitate understanding

Section 1.3

Scope of Business Analytics

Descriptive Analytics

The use of data to understand past and current business performance and make informed decisions.

Key Features:

  • Historical data analysis
  • Performance reporting
  • Dashboards and scorecards
  • Basic statistical methods
What happened?

Predictive Analytics

Predict the future by examining historical data, detecting patterns or relationships, and extrapolating these relationships forward in time.

Key Features:

  • Forecasting models
  • Machine learning algorithms
  • Regression analysis
  • Pattern recognition
What could happen?

Prescriptive Analytics

Identify the best alternatives to minimize or maximize some objective, recommending optimal actions.

Key Features:

  • Optimization models
  • Decision analysis
  • Simulation techniques
  • Business rules and constraints
What should we do?

The Analytics Spectrum

Past Focus
Descriptive
Predictive
Future Focus

Descriptive

Focuses on understanding what happened in the past to inform current decisions.

Predictive

Uses historical data to identify trends and predict future outcomes.

Prescriptive

Recommends actions to achieve desired outcomes and optimize performance.

Real-World Example: Amazon

Amazon demonstrates all three types of analytics working together to create a seamless customer experience:

Descriptive Analytics

Tracks user browsing history, purchase patterns, and inventory levels to understand current business state.

Predictive Analytics

Uses historical data to predict "Customers who bought this also bought..." and forecast demand for products.

Prescriptive Analytics

Optimizes warehouse locations, delivery routes, and pricing strategies to maximize efficiency and profit.

By integrating all three types of analytics, Amazon has revolutionized e-commerce and logistics, creating a customer-centric approach that anticipates needs and optimizes operations.

Big Data

Big data refers to massive amounts of business data (volume) from a wide variety of sources (variety), much of which is available in real time (velocity), and much of which is uncertain or unpredictable (veracity).

"The effective use of big data has the potential to transform economies, delivering a new wave of productivity growth and consumer surplus. Using big data will become a key basis of competition for existing companies, and will create new competitors who are able to attract employees that have the critical skills for a big data world."
McKinsey Global Institute, 2011

Volume

Massive amounts of data measured in terabytes to zettabytes

Variety

Structured, semi-structured, and unstructured data from diverse sources

Velocity

Speed at which data is generated, collected, and processed in real-time

Veracity

Uncertainty and unreliability inherent in some data sources

Data Reliability and Validity

Reliability

Data are accurate and consistent.

Reliability refers to whether a measurement instrument yields the same results each time it is used with the same subject under the same conditions.

Validity

Data correctly measures what it is supposed to measure.

Validity refers to how well a measurement instrument actually measures what it claims to measure and whether the data is useful for the intended purpose.

Tire Pressure Gauge

A tire pressure gauge that consistently reads several pounds of pressure below the true value is not reliable, although it is valid because it does measure tire pressure.

Not Reliable
Valid

Customer Service Calls

The number of calls to a customer service desk might be counted correctly each day (and thus is a reliable measure) but not valid if it is used to assess customer dissatisfaction, as many calls may be simple queries.

Reliable
Not Valid

Restaurant Survey

A survey question that asks a customer to rate the quality of the food in a restaurant may be neither reliable (because different customers may have conflicting perceptions) nor valid (if the intent is to measure customer satisfaction, as satisfaction generally includes other elements of service besides food).

Not Reliable
Not Valid
Applications

Examples of Business Analytics Applications

Business analytics is applied across various industries and functional areas to improve operations, enhance customer experiences, and drive strategic decisions.

Pricing

Setting optimal prices for consumer and industrial goods, government contracts, and maintenance contracts.

Example:

Airlines use dynamic pricing algorithms to adjust ticket prices based on demand, competitor prices, time to departure, and historical purchase patterns.

Customer Segmentation

Identifying and targeting key customer groups in retail, insurance, and credit card industries.

Example:

Credit card companies analyze spending patterns to create targeted promotions for specific customer segments based on lifestyle, income, and purchasing behavior.

Merchandising

Determining brands to buy, quantities, and allocations for retail operations.

Example:

Walmart analyzes sales data, seasonal trends, and local preferences to optimize inventory levels and product mix across thousands of stores.

Location

Finding the best location for bank branches and ATMs, or where to service industrial equipment.

Example:

Starbucks uses demographic data, traffic patterns, and competitor locations to determine optimal sites for new coffee shops.

Supply Chain Design

Determining the best sourcing and transportation options and finding the best delivery routes.

Example:

UPS uses advanced analytics to optimize delivery routes, reducing fuel consumption, emissions, and delivery times.

Staffing

Ensuring appropriate staffing levels and capabilities, and hiring the right people.

Example:

Google uses predictive analytics to identify candidate attributes that correlate with successful job performance and retention.

Healthcare

Scheduling operating rooms to improve utilization, improving patient flow and waiting times, purchasing supplies, and predicting health risk factors.

Example 1: Hospital Efficiency

Mayo Clinic uses analytics to optimize operating room scheduling, reducing waiting times and improving resource utilization.

Example 2: Predictive Diagnostics

Cleveland Clinic analyzes patient data to predict which patients are at higher risk for readmission, enabling preventive interventions.

Example 3: Supply Chain

Kaiser Permanente uses analytics to optimize medical supply inventory, reducing costs while ensuring critical supplies are always available.

Real-World Example: Target's Predictive Analytics

Target Corporation famously used predictive analytics to identify pregnant customers based on their purchasing patterns:

  • Target analyzed historical purchase data from customers who had signed up for baby registries
  • They identified 25 products that, when purchased together in specific patterns, strongly indicated a pregnancy
  • This allowed them to calculate a "pregnancy prediction score" and estimate due dates within a small window
  • Target used this information to send targeted advertisements for baby products to these customers

This case illustrates the power of predictive analytics, but also raises important ethical considerations about consumer privacy and the extent of data analysis that companies perform.
